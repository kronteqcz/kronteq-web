## Why automotive led the adhesive bonding revolution

The automotive industry was the first to adopt structural bonding as a manufacturing standard — and for a pragmatic reason: welding is not enough. The combination of dissimilar materials (steel + aluminium + plastic + glass + CFRP), pressure to reduce weight and NVH requirements created demand that only adhesive can answer.

Today an average passenger vehicle contains 15–20 kg of adhesives, sealants and tapes. Premium vehicles and electric cars significantly more.

## Body bonding: weld-bonding as standard

A modern vehicle body comprises dozens of steel, aluminium and plastic parts joined by a combination of spot welding and adhesive bonding. This combination — weld-bonding — increases body torsional stiffness by 20–40% compared to welding alone.

### How weld-bonding works in the body shop

1. A structural epoxy (crash-durable, typically PVC-based or toughened epoxy) is applied to steel or aluminium parts.
2. Parts are assembled into a jig.
3. Robotic welding guns perform spot welds — fixing position and providing immediate strength.
4. The body progresses along the line; adhesive cures in the cataphoresis bath (180 °C / 20–30 min) during the painting process.

Result: higher stiffness, better cabin vibration damping, better corrosion resistance at panel edges.

### Crashworthiness

For crash safety it is critical how the joint absorbs energy. A rigid adhesive would transfer force immediately and fracture. Crash-durable epoxies are toughened — they plastically deform during a crash and absorb energy rather than fracturing. This requirement is part of OEM and Tier 1 supplier design standards.

## Structural sealant glazing (SSG)

Modern vehicle glass is not screwed or clipped — it is bonded with structural PUR sealant. This is called *Structural Sealant Glazing* (SSG).

PUR sealant serves three functions:
- **Structural**: the glass contributes to body torsional stiffness — bonding integrates it as a load-bearing element
- **Sealing**: hermetic closure of the gap
- **Shock absorption**: the elastic layer damps vibrations and road impacts

### Parameters for automotive SSG

- PUR sealant strength: 3–8 MPa
- Elongation: 300–500%
- Cure: atmospheric moisture, 24–72 h
- Minimum strength for driving: 80–100% within 4–8 hours (the "safe drive away time")

## Plastic-to-metal bonding

Modern vehicles are full of plastic parts bonded to the metal body: spoilers, side mouldings, roof rails, pillar covers, bumpers. Welding is out of the question — plastics and metals cannot be welded.

### Technology solutions

**MMA acrylate adhesives**: Fast cure (5–30 min), good tolerance to imperfect plastic surfaces. Suitable for ABS, PC/ABS, glass-filled PA. PP and PE require a primer.

**Double-sided VHB tapes (Very High Bond)**: Invisible fastening construction. 3M VHB or equivalent tapes have shear strength comparable to liquid adhesive with significantly simpler application. Used for bonding emblems, decorative mouldings and interior panels.

## EV battery fixation

Electric vehicles have introduced a new type of bonding application: battery module fixation and thermal management. The battery is one of the largest and heaviest components — it must withstand vibration, impact, thermal cycling and simultaneously be serviceable in case of repair.

### Adhesive types for battery modules

**Thermally conductive adhesives and sealants (Thermal Interface Materials, TIM)**: Transfer heat from battery cells to the cooling system. Key parameters: thermal conductivity (0.5–5 W/(m·K)), electrical resistance, long-term thermal stability (−40 to +85 °C service, up to +150 °C short-term).

**Structural fixing adhesives**: Hold battery modules in the frame and transfer shear forces during vibration and impact. Typically two-component epoxies or PUR.

**Gap fillers**: Fill space between modules and the cooling plate, compensate tolerances and ensure planar thermal contact. Soft silicone or PUR foams, thermal conductivity 1–5 W/(m·K).

### Serviceability

One of the engineering challenges of EV batteries is the requirement for disassembly for service or recycling. "Debondable on demand" adhesives (responding to heat, induction or chemicals) are under development; currently the standard approach is constructional — combination of bonding with mechanical clamps.

## Trends: what is coming

**More aluminium and CFRP**: Lightweight materials require bonding. Weld-bonding for aluminium–steel transitions.

**Electrification**: More TIM adhesives, long-term thermal stability requirements, strict flammability standards (UL94).

**Dispensing automation**: Robotic dispensing heads with vision systems for 100% dispensing volume control.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can SSG glass be repaired without replacement?**
Not by standard methods. Repair of a cracked bonded windscreen requires replacement of both glass and sealant — demolition of the old bond, body cleaning, fresh primer coat, new PUR sealant and a safe drive away time of at least 4 hours.

**What standards regulate automotive bonding?**
Key industry standards include VDA 260 (adhesives in automotive), IATF 16949 (quality management system for the automotive supply chain), and OEM internal specifications (each vehicle manufacturer has their own standards for adhesive materials and processes).

**Are EV batteries more hazardous to service with bonded joints?**
Safety depends on the process, not the bonding method. HV battery service requires HV qualification regardless of joint type. Bonded battery fixation typically contributes to safety — it reduces mechanical vibration and protects against moisture ingress.
