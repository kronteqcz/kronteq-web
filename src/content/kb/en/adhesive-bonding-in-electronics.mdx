## Why electronics is different from automotive or construction

Adhesive bonding in electronics works with dimensions and tolerances in the order of microns, with materials that are extremely dissimilar (metal, glass, ceramic, rubber, PCB substrate) and with requirements unusual for other industries: electrical conductivity or insulation, thermal conductivity, optical transparency, or compatibility with soldering chemicals.

Simultaneously, electronics is an industry where miniaturisation never ends — and every product iteration requires re-evaluation of the bonding technology.

## Display fixation

Modern displays (LCD, OLED, AMOLED) are bonded into the device frame rather than mechanically attached. Reasons:
- Device thickness: screws and clips would add millimetres
- Display protection: adhesive absorbs impacts
- Sealing: IP53/IP68 requires hermetic sealing of the display perimeter
- Optical bonding: optically clear adhesive (OCA — Optically Clear Adhesive) eliminates the air gap between glass and display, improving sunlight readability

### OCA: optically clear acrylic adhesive

OCA (Optically Clear Adhesive) is a category of adhesives with light transmittance >99%, minimal scatter and extremely low yellowing after UV ageing. Used in layers 50–250 μm for glass and display bonding. Application is demanding — a single air bubble or dust particle is a defect visible to the user.

## Thermal management: thermally conductive adhesives

Power electronic components (power transistors, LED chips, IGBT power modules) generate heat that must be dissipated. The interface between chip and heatsink is a *Thermal Interface Material* (TIM).

### TIM types

**Thermally conductive adhesives**: Two-component epoxies or silicones with fillers (aluminium, aluminium oxide, boron nitrides, silver). Thermal conductivity: 0.8–5 W/(m·K). Permanently bond the heatsink — disassembly is destructive.

**Thermally conductive phase-change materials**: Solid at room temperature, soften at service temperature — self-filling air gaps. Reversible.

**Gap fillers**: Soft silicone or PUR foams for filling larger gaps (0.5–5 mm), thermal conductivity 1–5 W/(m·K). Standard in EV battery modules.

### Key parameters

- Thermal conductivity λ [W/(m·K)]
- Electrical conductivity or insulation (for power electronics generally insulation is required)
- Shrinkage during cure (affects thermo-mechanical stress)
- Service temperature (−40 °C to +150 °C for automotive, to +200 °C for industrial)

## Potting and encapsulation

Electronic boards and modules are protected by potting/encapsulation: filling into moulds or enclosures with a polymeric compound.

### Types of encapsulation systems

**Epoxies**: Hard encapsulation for rigid component fixation. Excellent thermal and chemical resistance. Unsuitable where disassembly is needed.

**Polyurethanes**: Flexible encapsulation — absorbs vibration and thermal expansion. Suitable for outdoor electronics.

**Silicones**: Thermally resistant and flexible. For applications with temperature peaks or where component flexibility is required (connectors, cables).

**Conformal coating**: Thin layer (25–130 μm) of epoxy, acrylate or silicone applied by spraying or dipping to PCBs. Protects against moisture, dust and condensation without fully fixing components.

## Environmental resistance

Electronics in industrial or automotive environments must meet stringent requirements:

**Humidity and condensation** (IEC 60068-2-78: 85 °C/85% RH/1000 h): Adhesives must maintain adhesion and insulation properties.

**Thermal shock** (−40 °C → +125 °C, repeatedly): Different thermal expansion coefficients of substrates and adhesive create shear stress — adhesive must be sufficiently elastic.

**Chemical resistance**: Cleaners, moisture, soldering flux — adhesive must resist the manufacturing and service processes.

**Vibration resistance** (IEC 60068-2-64): Adhesive fixes components and prevents mechanical fatigue of conductors and solder joints.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is thermally conductive adhesive necessary or does thermal paste suffice?**
Depends on requirements. Thermal paste is reversible and suitable for applications where heatsink replacement is needed. Thermally conductive adhesive is permanent but achieves lower thermal resistance through permanent contact. For automotive and industrial applications with vibration, adhesive or gap filler is preferred.

**Can OCA be applied manually?**
Theoretically yes, but industrial application requires cleanroom conditions (ISO class 7–8), a lamination machine and vacuum equipment for void elimination. Manual application is only suitable for prototypes and small series.

**How do I approach selecting an encapsulation system?**
Key questions: Is disassembly needed? (→ silicone or PUR). Is thermal conductivity the priority? (→ thermally conductive epoxy). Are UL/ATEX safety standards to be met? (→ specific formulation). Contact us with your requirements.
