## What is structural glazing?

Structural Sealant Glazing (SSG) is the method of attaching glass or metal facade panels to a support structure using structural sealant — without visible screws, rivets or mechanical clips.

The result is clean facades with an uninterrupted glass or aluminium surface, which have become the defining feature of modern commercial architecture. Behind this aesthetic lies decades of development in structural silicone and PUR sealants.

## Why adhesive instead of screws on a facade?

### Aesthetics
Screws, visible frames and mechanical clamps interrupt the visual continuity of the facade. SSG eliminates all visible external fasteners — the glass or panel appears to "float" in the aluminium frame.

### Movement and thermal expansion
A building is not rigid. Temperature changes, wind loading, expansion and settlement cause movement of facade elements. Silicone or PUR sealant is elastic — it compensates movements of ±15–25% of the joint width without loss of sealing or adhesion. Rigid adhesives or screws cannot handle this dynamic.

### Sealing and thermal insulation
The structural sealant simultaneously functions as a seal — preventing water, air and heat penetration. Joints are hermetically closed for the full designed service life.

## Types of sealants for SSG

### Silicone structural sealants

Silicone is the dominant technology for SSG — and for good reason:

- **Temperature resistance** −50 to +200 °C (briefly to +300 °C): facades must survive seasonal thermal cycling and direct solar heating
- **UV resistance**: silicone is not susceptible to photodegradation — it retains elasticity and adhesion for decades
- **Elasticity**: elongation 300–600%, elastic modulus 0.3–1.5 MPa — ideal for movement compensation
- **Durability**: service life >25 years in facade design (normative requirements ETAG 002, EOTA)

**Disadvantage**: Silicone has low tensile strength (1–5 MPa) — this is a limit for mechanically heavily loaded applications. Therefore for facades with extreme wind loading, silicone is combined with mechanical retention elements.

### PUR structural sealants

Where higher strength or faster cure is required, PUR sealant is an alternative. Strength 3–8 MPa, elongation 200–400%, UV resistance lower than silicone (UV-resistant topcoat or acceptance of colour change required). Suitable for joints in industrial buildings, hangars and warehouses.

### MS polymer sealants

MS (Modified Silicone or Methyl Silyl-terminated Polyether) polymers combine the elasticity and UV resistance of silicone with better adhesion to coating systems and lower cost. Increasingly used for less exposed joints and lighter facade applications.

## Facade system applications

### Curtain wall

A suspended facade is a load-bearing system where glass or metal panels are carried by an aluminium sub-structure attached to the building's structural frame. SSG sealant bonds the panel to the sub-structure — the panel requires no visible fasteners.

Critical: the panel must transfer wind loading (both pressure and suction) to the sub-structure. Sealant joint width dimensioning accounts for sealant strength, panel area and local wind loading.

### Structural glass fins

Glass fins are vertical load-bearing elements of glass bonded to horizontal beams. The entire structure is glass — an aesthetically extreme solution for entrance halls and atria. Strength dimensioning is critical; requires precise calculation of sealant strength under permanent loading (creep).

### Triple and quadruple glazing

Modern energy-efficient buildings use triple or even quadruple glazing. The panes are bonded at the perimeter by a spacer bar; structural sealant seals the outer side of the joint. The secondary sealant must resist UV, moisture and thermal cycling throughout the window service life (>25 years normatively).

## Surface preparation — construction specifics

For SSG strict preparation rules apply:

1. **Cleaning**: Clean aluminium, anodised or powder-coated. Degrease with IPA or specialist cleaner.
2. **Primer**: For anodised or powder-coated aluminium a primer for silicone is essential — without primer adhesion is unreliable.
3. **Substrate test**: Before full-scale application, always perform an adhesion test on a small area — cut and peel after 24 h.
4. **Climatic conditions**: Most structural sealants must not be applied below +5 °C or above +40 °C, or at relative humidity >80%.

## Durability: normative requirements

Structural sealants for SSG are regulated by ETAG 002 (European Technical Approval Guideline for Structural Sealant Glazing) and EN 13022. They require:

- Artificial ageing tests (UV, humidity, thermal cycling)
- Declaration of designed service life (typically 25 years)
- Inspection and maintenance recommendations (typically every 5 years)

## Frequently asked questions

**How long does silicone sealant last on a facade?**
High-quality structural silicone sealants have a designed service life of 25–30 years with correct application and regular inspection. Degradation is signalled by cracking, colour change or loss of elasticity.

**Can the sealant alone carry the weight of a glass panel?**
Depends on the system design. SSG standardly transfers wind loading (horizontal) through the sealant; gravity (vertical) is carried by mechanical support elements at the bottom edge of the panel. Fully structural systems without support elements are exceptions and require specialist calculation.

**Is bonded facade glazing repairable?**
Local repair of cracked sealant is possible — the damaged sealant is removed, fresh surface preparation is carried out and new sealant is applied. But adhesion of new sealant to old silicone is complicated — silicone does not wet to further silicone without specialist primer.
