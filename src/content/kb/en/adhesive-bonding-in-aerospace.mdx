## Why aerospace adopted bonding first

The aerospace industry was the pioneer of structural bonding — and for good reason: every kilogram of extra weight costs fuel for the entire service life of the aircraft. Riveting creates holes in the material, stress concentrations around holes and adds weight from the rivets themselves. Bonding eliminates holes, uniformly distributes stress and reduces weight.

The first commercially successful applications date from the 1940s–50s (de Havilland Hornet, Fokker). Today bonding is standard on all modern commercial aircraft.

## Composites: the material where bonding has no alternative

Carbon composites (CFRP — Carbon Fibre Reinforced Polymer) are the material of today's aerospace: the Boeing 787 is >50% CFRP, the Airbus A350 >53%. Why? Density ~1.6 g/cm³ versus ~2.7 g/cm³ for aluminium, with comparable or higher strength.

Composites cannot be welded. They are inhomogeneous and anisotropic — thermal processing is destructive. **The only method for joining composite-to-composite and composite-to-metal is bonding (or riveting).**

### Where bonding is used in aerospace composites

- **Sandwich panels**: Two thin CFRP skins bonded to a lightweight core (Nomex honeycomb, PUR foam). The result is an extremely stiff and light panel — the foundation of floors, walls and bulkheads.
- **Bonded ribs and stringers**: Stiffening elements (ribs, longitudinal stringers) bonded to the outer skin of the wing or fuselage.
- **System installations**: Electrical conduits, hydraulics, protective covers — all fixed by adhesive or tape to prevent vibration and fretting.

## Rivet-bonding in aluminium structures

For aluminium parts (the remaining ~30–40% of a modern aircraft structure) the standard is *rivet-bonding*: the combination of adhesive and rivet.

### Why rivet-bonding in aerospace?

Aluminium is durable but fatigue-sensitive. Rivet holes are the sites of fatigue crack initiation. Research has shown that rivet-bonding extends joint fatigue life by 2–5× compared to riveting alone.

**Mechanism**: Adhesive fills the gap between aluminium parts and around the rivet hole. It eliminates fretting (microscopic movement at the interface under dynamic loading), which is the primary cause of fatigue crack initiation.

### Epoxy adhesives for aerospace applications

Aerospace epoxies are two-component systems with exceptional parameters:
- Tensile strength: 50–80 MPa
- Shear strength: 25–35 MPa
- Temperature resistance: 120–180 °C (specialist to 200 °C)
- Resistance to fuel, hydraulic fluids and deicers
- Compliance with normative requirements (FAA, EASA material specification)

## Certification: the critical difference from industry

In aerospace you cannot simply "pick an adhesive from a catalogue". Every material used in primary or secondary structure must be certified or approved within:

- **CMH-17** (Composite Materials Handbook) — standard methods for composites
- **FAA AC 21.303** or **EASA CS-25** — structural material certification
- **OEM internal approval** (Boeing, Airbus, Embraer) — each aircraft manufacturer maintains an approved materials list (AMM — Aircraft Maintenance Manual)

The material certification process includes extensive mechanical testing, thermal cycling (−55 °C to +85 °C), climate ageing, chemical exposure tests and simulated operational loading.

## Validation requirements in practice

For aerospace applications validation is a process, not a one-time test:

1. **Qualified Material**: Adhesive must appear on the aircraft manufacturer's approved list.
2. **Qualified Process**: Surface preparation and application procedure must be documented and validated.
3. **Qualified Personnel**: Operators must be trained and certified for the specific process.
4. **First Article Inspection**: The first production run must pass the full test scope.
5. **In-process control**: Process parameters (temperature, humidity, time, dispensing) are continuously monitored.

## Trends: electric and composite future

**eVTOL and UAM**: Electric vertical take-off and landing vehicles (Urban Air Mobility) are largely bonded — composite frame, bonded batteries, multi-material integrated structures.

**Additive manufacturing + bonding**: 3D-printed metal and polymer parts are integrated into bonded composite assemblies.

**Autonomous bonding inspection**: Robotic NDT systems with AI analysis are replacing manual ultrasonic scanning for large areas.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is bonding safer than riveting in primary aerospace structure?**
Both technologies are certifiable for primary structure with correct design, validation and process control. Pure bonded primary joints are less common (complex validation, conservative regulatory approach); rivet-bonding combinations are the standard.

**How are bonded joints in aircraft inspected in service?**
Aircraft undergo regular C-check and D-check maintenance inspections. For critical bonded areas NDT intervals are defined (ultrasound, thermography). Visual inspection reveals surface cracks and edge delaminations on panels.

**Can aerospace adhesives be used in industry?**
Technically yes — but for industrial applications it is unnecessarily expensive and complex. Aerospace epoxies have outstanding parameters but high cost and complex processing. For industrial applications equivalent or sufficient aerospace-inspired systems are available at lower cost.
