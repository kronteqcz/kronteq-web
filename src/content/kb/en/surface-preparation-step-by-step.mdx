## Why surface preparation determines 90% of the result

Industrial studies and warranty claim analyses repeatedly show the same pattern: 9 out of 10 adhesive bond failures have their cause in inadequate surface preparation — not in the wrong adhesive. The adhesive is most often fine. The surface is not.

Investment in a more expensive adhesive without ensuring surface preparation quality is wasted money. Conversely: an average adhesive with excellent surface preparation will outperform a premium adhesive on a poorly prepared surface.

## Step 1: Mechanical cleaning

**Goal:** Remove loose layers (rust, oxides, old coating, corrosion products) and increase effective contact area by roughening the surface.

### Methods

**Grinding** (abrasive paper P80–P320) is the most accessible method for metals and wood. Always in one direction — along the joint length, never in circular motions (circular marks concentrate stress). Resulting roughness Ra 2–6 μm is optimal for most structural adhesives.

**Sandblasting / grit blasting** (steel shot, corundum, glass beads) is the industrial standard for large areas or series production. Achieves cleanliness Sa 2.5 per ISO 8501 (less than 10% visible contamination) or Sa 3 (clean metal). Creates an "anchor profile" — a micro-porous structure the adhesive mechanically keys into.

**Note on plastics:** On plastics (PP, PE, ABS) abrasive cleaning is counterproductive without subsequent activation — the abrasive embeds contamination from the grit into the surface. For plastics it is better to proceed directly to activation.

### Most common mistake

After mechanical cleaning, do not touch the surface with bare hands. The fatty perspiration from skin (acids, oils) immediately re-contaminates the surface, negating all prior work. Use clean cotton or nitrile gloves.

## Step 2: Chemical degreasing

**Goal:** Remove oils, greases, machining fluid emulsions and fingerprints. Achieve wettability — a drop of water or IPA must spread into a flat disc.

### Selecting the degreaser

**IPA (isopropanol, 2-propanol)** is the most universal industrial degreaser. Safe for metals, plastics and lacquers, fast evaporation (boiling point 82 °C), does not oxidise metals. Effective against light oils and fingerprints. Insufficient for heavy machining oils.

**Acetone** is a stronger solvent than IPA — better at dissolving fatty contaminants. Unsuitable for painted surfaces and plastics sensitive to ketones (polycarbonate, PMMA). Fast evaporation.

**Specialist cleaners** based on esters or water are suitable for automated application (immersion cleaners, ultrasonic). Odourless, easier handling in production.

### Correct wiping technique

The most common mistake: two cloths simultaneously. The technician takes two rags — wiping with the first and polishing with the second. The result is the contamination being smeared across the surface.

**Correct procedure:**
1. Spray degreaser or saturate cloth.
2. Wipe in *one direction* — one pass along the full length.
3. Discard used cloth.
4. Fresh cloth for a confirmatory wipe.

Use only cotton (lint-free) cloths or specialist industrial wipes. Microfibre leaves residues; paper leaves fibres.

### Cleanliness test

Water break test: apply a drop of deionised water (or IPA) to the degreased surface. If it spreads into a flat disc (contact angle &lt;30°), the surface is clean. If it contracts into a bead, it is still contaminated.

For more precise measurement, surface energy test pens (dyne pens) are available for ranges 30–72 mN/m. The adhesive needs a surface with energy of at least 38 mN/m, ideally >48 mN/m.

## Step 3: Surface activation

**Goal:** Increase surface energy above 48–56 mN/m. For difficult-to-bond substrates, create chemical bonds between primer and substrate.

### Plastics: when activation is mandatory

PP (polypropylene) and PE (polyethylene) have inherent surface energy of 29–31 mN/m. The adhesive does not spread, does not wet, does not grip — adhesion is minimal. Without activation, the bond is unreliable.

PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene) is an extreme case: surface energy ~18 mN/m. Without special treatment (sodium etching or plasma) nothing bonds to it.

### Activation methods

**Primer** is a chemical applied by wipe or spray. PP primer is typically chlorinated polyolefin or organosilane — it creates chemical bonds with the otherwise inert surface layer. Metal primer acts as a corrosion inhibitor and adhesion promoter.

Key rules:
- Apply a minimal quantity (thin layer, not puddles)
- Allow to dry (typically 1–5 min — depends on formulation)
- Bond before the primer dries completely or carbonates

**Plasma** is a stream of ionised gas (air, nitrogen, argon). Cleans and activates the surface in one step — removes organic contaminants and oxidises the surface, raising surface energy. Plastics reach >70 mN/m after plasma.

Effect lasts 20–60 minutes for plastics, shorter for metals (re-oxidation). Plasma is a significant investment but the only consistent method for difficult-to-bond plastics in series production.

**Flame treatment** is a more accessible alternative to plasma for PP, PE and HDPE. Results depend on flame intensity and distance — requires strict parameterisation. In series production robotic execution is required.

**Corona** is electrical discharge for films, tapes and flat materials. Standard in bonding tape and packaging material manufacture.

### Time window after activation

| Method | Typical window |
|---|---|
| IPA degreasing (metals) | &lt;30 min (oxidation) |
| IPA degreasing (plastics) | &lt;60 min |
| Plasma (plastics) | 20–60 min |
| Primer (metals) | 5–30 min after drying |
| Primer (plastics) | 10 min–24 h depending on formulation |

After the window expires, degreasing must be repeated.

## Most common production mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Wiping without changing cloth | Contamination smeared across surface |
| Touching with bare hands | Skin oils re-contaminate surface |
| Waiting too long after activation | Surface degrades, adhesion drops |
| Grinding plastics without activation | Grit embeds contamination into microstructure |
| Using acetone on painted surface | Lacquer damage, adhesion failure |

## Frequently asked questions

**How do I check surface energy without a laboratory?**
Test pens (dyne pens, available from manufacturers such as Enercon, Tantec) are a pocket solution. If the liquid from the pen holds on the surface without contracting, surface energy is ≥ the pen's value.

**Do I need to degrease a new part straight from production?**
Yes. Machining oils, anti-corrosion agents, release waxes and mould release agents (for plastic mouldings) are always present and must be removed.

**How does degreasing differ for different metals?**
Aluminium is reactive — after IPA degreasing it oxidises lightly; bond within 30 minutes or use an activator. Stainless steel is more stable — the window after IPA degreasing is longer. Galvanised surfaces require a specialist primer for zinc.
