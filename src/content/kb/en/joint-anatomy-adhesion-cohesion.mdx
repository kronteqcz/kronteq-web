## Two principles behind every bond

Every adhesive bond depends on two independently acting forces. Understanding their difference is the key to why bonds hold — and why they fail.

**Adhesion** is the bonding of the adhesive to the substrate surface. It arises from physical forces (van der Waals interactions, capillary wetting) and chemical bonds. Adhesion depends on how well the surface is prepared: clean, wettable, with sufficient surface energy.

**Cohesion** is the internal strength of the adhesive — how strongly it holds its own molecules together. This depends on the polymer's chemical structure, degree of cross-linking and fillers. Epoxies have high cohesion and are rigid. Polyurethanes have lower cohesion but high elongation.

These two forces are independent. You can have excellent cohesion (strong adhesive) and catastrophic adhesion (adhesive peels cleanly from the surface). They cannot substitute for each other.

## The weakest link principle

A joint always fails at the point of lowest strength — this is physical law, not hypothesis.

Two types of failure typically occur:
- **Cohesive failure**: the adhesive tears within its own layer. Both surfaces are covered with adhesive residue. This is generally a good sign — it means adhesion is strong, but the adhesive is too rigid or overloaded.
- **Adhesive failure**: the adhesive peels completely from one surface, which is clean. This is a bad sign — it means adhesion failure, almost always caused by inadequate surface preparation.

In practice there is also mixed failure (combination of both). The ideal result of a destructive test is cohesive failure — it indicates the system is working correctly.

## Why 90% of failures occur at the interface

Industrial warranty claim analyses and scientific studies repeatedly agree: **in 90% of adhesive bond failures the cause was inadequate surface preparation**, not a poor adhesive.

Mechanisms:
1. **Oils and greases**: A nanometre-thin oil film (invisible to the eye) radically reduces wettability. The adhesive does not spread — adhesion is near zero.
2. **Oxides**: The oxide layer on aluminium or steel is mechanically weak and brittle. The adhesive bonds to the oxide, but the oxide then peels from the base metal.
3. **Low surface energy**: Plastics such as PP and PE have inherently low surface energy (&lt;30 mN/m). Without primer or plasma the adhesive does not spread — it beads up like water on a flat leaf.
4. **Moisture**: Water in the substrate environment creates an adsorbed layer that competes with the adhesive for adhesion.

## Joint geometry determines where stress concentrates

Joint shape determines how loading is distributed — and where critical points arise. Three basic types:

**Lap joint**: simple overlap of two surfaces. Most common in industry. Stress concentrates at the overlap edges — stress transfer is lower in the middle. Overlap length must be optimised.

**Butt joint**: parts face-to-face. Transfers tensile and compressive stress well; peel strength is low. Unsuitable for dynamic loading.

**T-joint**: adhesive must transfer both tensile, shear and peel forces. Most critical geometry; requires adhesive with good elongation.

## Layer thickness: why 0.1–0.3 mm

The optimum adhesive layer thickness for structural adhesives is typically 0.1–0.3 mm. Too thin a layer (below 0.05 mm) increases stress peaks and reduces the ability to compensate geometric tolerances. Too thick a layer (above 0.5 mm) reduces shear strength — cohesion becomes the limiting factor.

Air bubbles and unbonded areas are defects equivalent to poor surface preparation: they locally eliminate adhesion and create a crack initiation site.

## Practical implications

1. Investment in a better adhesive is less effective than investment in better surface preparation.
2. Adhesive failure (clean surface after peeling) = immediate production stop and audit of degreasing/activation process.
3. Cohesive failure = system is working, optimise adhesive selection or joint geometry.
4. Every new bond must be validated by destructive testing — visual inspection is insufficient.

## Frequently asked questions

**How do I identify the failure type without a laboratory?**
Visually: adhesive failure = one surface is completely clean. Cohesive failure = adhesive residue on both surfaces. Mixed failure = combination. This basic assessment is within any production engineer's capability.

**Can too much adhesive cause harm?**
Yes. Excess adhesive creates unnecessary stress at joint edges, adds weight and in the case of reactive adhesives (epoxies) can cause overheating during exothermic cure (for large volumes).

**What is surface energy and how do I measure it?**
Surface energy (in mN/m) expresses a surface's ability to accept a liquid. It is measured by water contact angle or specialist test pens (available for ranges 30–70 mN/m). The adhesive needs a surface with energy at least 10 mN/m higher than its own surface tension.
