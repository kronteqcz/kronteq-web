## Bonding can do a lot — but not everything

Structural bonding is a powerful technology, but like any other it has limits. Knowing them is part of a professional approach. Ignoring them leads to expensive warranty claims.

## Limit 1: Temperature

Every adhesive has a temperature window outside which its properties degrade. Two critical temperatures exist:

**Glass transition temperature (Tg)**: For amorphous polymers (epoxies, acrylates) this is the temperature above which the polymer transitions from rigid to rubbery state — strength drops dramatically. For standard epoxies Tg is typically 80–120 °C.

**Maximum service temperature**: The temperature up to which the adhesive maintains its declared mechanical properties under continuous loading.

| Adhesive type | Standard max. service temperature | Specialist formulations |
|---|---|---|
| Cyanoacrylate (CA) | 80 °C | 120 °C |
| PUR | 120 °C | 150 °C |
| MMA acrylate | 100–120 °C | — |
| Epoxy | 120–150 °C | 200–300 °C |
| Silicone | 200 °C | 300 °C |
| MS polymer | 100 °C | — |

**Note on temperature peaks**: Short peaks (e.g. paint oven curing at 180 °C / 20 min) can be tolerated by specialist formulations designed for this purpose. Standard adhesives cannot.

**Temperature cycling** (alternating temperatures) is more aggressive than constant temperature. Different coefficients of thermal expansion of substrate and adhesive create repeated shear stress — particularly critical for rigid epoxies without elasticity.

## Limit 2: Ageing — UV and moisture

Polymers age. Degradation intensity depends on the environment.

**UV radiation**: Acrylates and PUR without UV stabilisers are sensitive to photodegradation — they become brittle and crack. UV-stabilised formulations or protective coatings are essential for outdoor applications. Silicones and some specialist epoxies are inherently UV-resistant.

**Moisture**: Water degrades adhesion by two mechanisms:
1. Hydrophilic diffusion — water penetrates to the adhesive–substrate interface and weakens adhesive bonds
2. Hydrolysis — water chemically breaks bonds in the adhesive layer

Moisture resistance depends on formulation. For permanently wet environments (bathrooms, swimming pools, marine construction) silicones, specialist MS polymers or PUR with hydrophobic additives are appropriate.

**Heat + humidity** (85 °C / 85% RH) is the most aggressive combination for adhesive ageing — the standard in IEC 60068-2-78 for electronic applications.

## Limit 3: Joint permanence and disassembly

Bonded joints are permanent. This is both an advantage (no bolt loosening, no fastener corrosion) and a disadvantage (repairs and recycling are more difficult).

**Disassembly** is destructive: requires heat (above Tg the material softens), abrasive tools, or chemical softener. None of the methods is non-invasive to both joint members.

**Recyclability**: In automotive, recyclability is an increasing concern. Research is directed towards "debondable on demand" adhesives — reactive adhesives that can be deliberately deactivated by electrical impulse or induction. In industrial practice this remains an exception.

**Field repairability**: For applications where field repair is necessary (construction machinery, vehicles), it is advisable to choose a combination of bonding + mechanical fasteners (hybrid joint), where the fastener can be replaced without destroying the entire joint.

## Limit 4: NDT — inspecting the invisible

Visual inspection reveals only:
- Adhesive squeeze-out at edges (shows that adhesive was applied)
- Visible surface cracks

What visual inspection *does not* reveal:
- Air voids inside the adhesive layer
- Insufficient wetting (dry spots)
- Sub-surface delamination
- Adhesion degradation caused by ageing

### NDT methods for bonded joints

**Ultrasonic scanning (UT)** is the most widely used method. The ultrasonic beam passes through the joint and its reflections reveal interfaces and defects. Detects voids, dry spots and large delaminations. Limitation: cannot reliably detect "kissing bonds" (joints where surfaces are in contact but adhesion is low).

**Thermographic analysis (lock-in thermography)** uses induced heat or vibration and monitors thermal response. Defect areas (voids, dry spots) have different thermal response. Suitable for large areas; more sensitive to kissing bonds than UT.

**Laser shearography** measures surface deformation under loading. Detects sub-surface defects. Used in aerospace for composite panels.

**Tap test** is simple and immediate: tapping with a coin or hammer can distinguish a hollow (delaminated) sound from a solid (intact bond) sound. Subjective; dependent on operator experience.

### Series production quality control

For series production the most practical approach combines:
1. **Process control** (surface preparation parameters, dispensing, cure) — preventive
2. **Statistical sampling** (destructive tensile/peel tests from each production batch) — detective
3. **Selective NDT** for safety-critical applications or after process anomalies

## Frequently asked questions

**Must I test every bonded joint?**
In series production 100% NDT is generally uneconomical. The standard is process control (dispensing, surface preparation) + statistical sampling of destructive tests. For safety-critical applications (aerospace, rescue equipment) the scope of NDT must be defined with the certification authority.

**How do I know the adhesive has reached full strength?**
Most structural adhesives reach 50–80% of final strength within 24 hours at room temperature, 100% within 7 days. Thermal cure (80–150 °C) reduces this to hours. Practical verification: destructive testing of a sample from the same batch after the planned cure.

**Can adhesive ageing cause failure without visible signs?**
Yes — adhesion degradation (loss of adhesion) can occur without visible surface cracks. It may only manifest under mechanical loading. That is why regular inspections (NDT or destructive sampling) are essential for critical applications.
