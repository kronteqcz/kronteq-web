## Why adhesive selection is not trivial

Catalogues contain hundreds of products. Every manufacturer claims their adhesive is the best. In reality there is no adhesive that is best in all applications — there is only the adhesive that is right for *your specific* application.

Correct selection depends on five dimensions: **strength**, **flexibility**, **temperature resistance**, **chemical resistance** and **cure speed**. Every adhesive is a compromise.

## Scatter diagram: strength × elongation

The most intuitive overview is the distribution of adhesive types on the axes of strength (MPa) vs. flexibility (% elongation):

| Type | Strength (MPa) | Elongation (%) | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cyanoacrylate (CA) | 10–25 | &lt;10% | Rigid, brittle, fast |
| Epoxy | 70–90 | 50–200% | Strong, rigid |
| MMA acrylate | 40–70 | 100–400% | Strong, semi-flexible |
| Polyurethane (PUR) | 20–40 | 400–800% | Elastic, flexible |
| Silicone | 1–5 | 200–600% | Soft, extremely elastic |

The strength and flexibility axes are approximately inversely correlated: the stronger, the more rigid.

## Epoxies: maximum strength, minimum elasticity

**When to use:** Statically loaded structural joints requiring strength or temperature resistance. Electronics, aerospace, industrial machinery.

**Key parameters:**
- Strength 70–90 MPa — highest of standard adhesives
- Temperature resistance: standard to 120 °C, specialist to 300 °C
- Cure: 5 min (fast formulations) to 24 h at room temperature; thermal cure (~80–150 °C) significantly increases final strength
- Chemical resistance: excellent (resistance to acids, solvents, oils)

**Disadvantages:** Brittleness — under impact loading they can crack. Sensitivity of the two-component system to precise mix ratio.

**Toughened epoxies** are compromise formulations: strength 40–60 MPa, elongation 200–400%. Used where impact resistance is needed alongside structural strength (automotive crash-durable adhesives).

## Polyurethanes (PUR): flexibility and NVH

**When to use:** Dynamically loaded joints, joints subject to vibration or impact, glass bonding, joints at risk of peel loading.

**Key parameters:**
- Elongation 400–800% — absorbs vibration and impact
- Strength 20–40 MPa — lower than epoxies but sufficient for many applications
- One-component PUR: cures with atmospheric moisture (1–72 h depending on layer thickness and humidity)
- Two-component PUR: cures by mixing components, speed controllable by formulation

**Where they dominate:** Automotive SSG (structural sealant glazing), industrial floors, facade systems, panel bonding with vibration exposure.

**Note:** One-component PUR reacts with atmospheric moisture during storage — an opened tube must be stored with a sealing cap. Before curing they are sensitive to substrate moisture.

## MMA acrylates: speed + contamination tolerance

**When to use:** Fast assembly, substrates that cannot be perfectly degreased, or where plastics, metals and composites must be joined without primer.

**Key parameters:**
- Strength 40–70 MPa — comparable to epoxies
- Cure 5–30 min (exothermic reaction)
- Tolerance to slight oil contamination — exceptional property
- Do not bond PE or PP without primer

**Where they dominate:** Agricultural and construction machinery, buses and trams, composite panels, industrial covers.

**Note:** Strong odour during application (methyl ester) — ventilation or respirator required. Precise mix ratio must be maintained.

## Cyanoacrylates (CA): seconds for small areas

**When to use:** Small areas, rapid component fixation, electronics assembly, prototyping.

**Key parameters:**
- Cure 5–60 s
- Strength 10–25 MPa, but *low peel strength*
- Temperature limit ~80 °C (specialist formulations 120 °C)

**Why not for structural applications:** Brittle, moisture-sensitive on ageing, low peel strength. For joints with larger areas or dynamic loading they are unsuitable.

## Silicones: sealing and thermal resistance

**When to use:** Sealing, flexible joints, environments with high temperature or UV.

**Key parameters:**
- Strength 1–5 MPa — unsuitable for structural bonds
- Temperature resistance −60 to +300 °C — best of all
- UV resistance — excellent

**Where they dominate:** Engine bay sealing, ovens, electrical enclosures, structural glazing (combined with PUR for strength).

## Decision tree

1. Is maximum strength and/or temperature resistance the priority? → **Epoxy**
2. Is flexibility, NVH damping or glass bonding the priority? → **PUR**
3. Is fast cure (5–30 min) or contamination tolerance the priority? → **MMA acrylate**
4. Small area (&lt;5 cm²) and time is critical? → **Cyanoacrylate (CA)**
5. Is it sealing or environment >150 °C? → **Silicone**
6. Unsure? → Consult specific requirements →

## Five key questions before selection

1. **What materials am I joining?** (metal + metal, metal + plastic, composite)
2. **What is the service environment?** (temperature, humidity, UV, chemicals)
3. **What is the mechanical loading?** (static, dynamic, vibration, impact)
4. **What is the production process?** (cure time, application method, volume)
5. **Are there disassembly requirements?** (permanent joint vs. serviceable)

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I substitute MMA for epoxy when I need faster cure?**
Yes — MMA cures in 5–30 min versus 24 h for epoxy at RT. Strength is comparable, but MMA is less temperature resistant (to ~120 °C versus 150+ °C for epoxies). Suitable for most automotive and industrial applications without thermal stress.

**How does temperature affect adhesive selection?**
Temperature resistance is a parameter that can never be exceeded. Determine the maximum service temperature of the application and select an adhesive with a 20–30 °C safety margin. Temperature peaks (short-term) differ from continuous service temperatures.

**What is a two-component adhesive and how do I apply it?**
A two-component (2K) adhesive consists of component A (resin/isocyanate) and component B (hardener/polyol). They are mixed immediately before application — manual mixing in a cartridge gun, or a robotic dispensing system with a static mixer.
